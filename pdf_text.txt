Abstract (Executive Summary)

This Software Design Document outlines the architectural blueprint for EZShop, a modern

e-commerce clothing platform. As online retail marketing grows increasingly sophisticated,
traditional backend systems often accumulate severe technical debt when attempting to

process overlapping promotional campaigns and diverse payment methods. Poorly designed

pricing engines frequently degrade into deeply nested conditional statements and tightly

coupled dependencies, violating core software engineering principles and hindering future
scalability.


To resolve these architectural bottlenecks, this project proposes a robust, scalable backend
infrastructure that strictly adheres to object-oriented programming standards and the SOLID

principles. The proposed solution is engineered around three foundational Gang of Four

(GoF) design patterns to achieve a strict separation of concerns:

    1. The Decorator and Strategy Patterns (Pricing Engine): The system combines

       these two patterns to create a dynamic pricing calculator. The Decorator pattern

       allows the system to recursively "wrap" a shopping cart in multiple promotional
       layers (e.g., stacking a 20% discount with an RM 5 shipping voucher) without

       triggering a subclass explosion. Simultaneously, the Strategy pattern encapsulates the

       specific mathematical algorithms for these discounts, allowing new promotional rules

       to be integrated dynamically at runtime.
    2. The Strategy Pattern (Payment Processing): To abstract the checkout workflow

       from external dependencies, the system utilizes the Strategy pattern to define a family

       of interchangeable payment algorithms (Credit Card, E-Wallet, and Online Banking),
       allowing seamless transaction routing without modifying the core checkout context.

    3. The Observer Pattern (Order Notifications): To ensure the checkout flow remains

       fast and responsive, the Observer pattern decouples the main transaction sequence
       from secondary post-purchase tasks. Upon a successful payment, the system

       automatically broadcasts state changes to registered observers, triggering independent

       background processes such as inventory deduction and email receipt dispatch.

Ultimately, this document validates that by encapsulating variant algorithms, dynamically

composing object behaviors, and decoupling state notifications, the EZShop architecture
provides a highly maintainable, easily testable, and extensible foundation capable of adapting
to future e-commerce business requirements.



1. Introduction



1.1. Abstract

With the rapid advancement of technology, online e-commerce stores are more plentiful than
ever. As a result, online shopping has become the norm for everyday people, owing to the

convenience and ease of the online shopping experience. This is especially true in clothing
retail, as customers can browse the massive collections of clothing of a particular shop more

efficiently than travelling to the physical store.


A major selling point of e-commerce clothing shops is the abundance of discounts and

vouchers. These discounts are often offered as part of a broader promotional campaign by the

retail company to boost sales and drive engagement. However, as marketing campaigns grow
more complex, the same applies for the discount schemes and benefits. For example, many

brands offer membership tiers, with each tier offering a certain percentage of discounts on
purchases, while also offering seasonal discounts and rebates for referrals. Although these

discount schemes are the backbone of e-commerce clothing systems, the backend logic can

get complex and convoluted if the architecture is poorly designed. This incurs a significant
amount of technical debt, slowing down development and making the system difficult to

understand. This has devastating consequences for businesses because more time is spent on

dissecting the architecture instead of actually working on improving the system.


The proposed e-commerce clothing system aims to address this pain point. The system
(“EZShop”) is designed to be able to handle multiple discount types while allowing new

schemes to be integrated seamlessly into the system. This document serves as the Software

Design Proposal and Software Design Pattern Document for EZShop.


1.2. Problem Statement

As e-commerce marketing campaigns grow more sophisticated with time, which include
discount codes, referral discounts, seasonal rebates and so on, the complexities of handling

multiple types of discounts causes the code to deviate from programming best practices. The
code for calculating these discounts often result in nested conditionals and tight coupling.

Consequently, software design principles are violated, most notably the Single Responsibility
Principle and Open-Closed Principle, causing the system to be unmaintainable and difficult to

modify or extend without inadvertently breaking existing functionalities. This would not only

make it hard to refactor the existing codebase, but would also make testing extremely
challenging. For example, unit testing would be nearly impossible due to the tightly-coupled

nature of a poorly-designed architecture, making debugging significantly more difficult.


Hence, a viable solution is needed to solve these architectural issues to circumvent this

bottleneck and ensure maintainability.



1.3. Project Objectives
The main goal of the project is to design, architect and develop a robust and scalable

architecture for EZShop, an e-commerce platform for selling clothing. More specifically, this

project aims to achieve the following objectives:

    ● To design a backend architecture for EZShop that encompasses all core retail
        functions, including product catalog management, shopping cart operations, checkout

        processing, order management, inventory control, and customer notification, ensuring

        that each subsystem has clearly defined responsibilities and boundaries in accordance
        with the Single Responsibility Principle (SRP).

    ● To design a backend architecture that is capable of processing multiple types of

        discount schemes and payment types while maintaining a clear separation of concerns
        between the discount calculation logic and the checkout flow.

    ● To implement the Strategy Pattern in the Payment Processing module, enabling the

        system to support multiple payment methods, including credit card, online banking,

        and e-wallet, as interchangeable algorithms that can be selected at runtime without
        modifying the core checkout flow. This will directly enforce the Open/Closed

        Principle (OCP), ensuring new payment methods can be introduced without

        regression risk to existing functionality.
    ● To implement the Observer Pattern in the Order Notification subsystem, enabling

        multiple independent post-purchase processes: inventory stock deduction and

        customer email confirmation, to be triggered automatically upon order completion.
        This ensures that the checkout module remains entirely decoupled from its
       downstream side effects, allowing new post-purchase processes to be registered

       without altering the core order processing logic.
    ● To implement the Decorator Pattern in the Pricing Engine, enabling multiple

       independent discount types, that is, percentage-based seasonal sales and fixed amount

       vouchers to be stacked dynamically at runtime without relying on a rigid inheritance
       hierarchy.



1.4. Literature Review

Design patterns have become a foundational pillar of modern software engineering, ever
since they were popularized by Erich Gamma, Richard Helm, Ralph Johnson and John

Vlissides, collectively known as the Gang of Four (GoF), in their book Design Patterns:

Elements of Reusable Object-Oriented Software. Design patterns provide standardized and
reusable solutions to common software design challenges; they also serve as a blueprint for

developers to build systems that are maintainable and scalable. The GoF proposed 23 patterns

across three categories: creational, structural and behavioural, whereby each category is

designed to address distinct design issues. In the case of an e-commerce platform like
EZShop, discount calculation rules evolve rapidly in response to marketing campaigns,

behavioral patterns that encapsulate variable algorithms are particularly relevant. This

literature review examines three sources: the foundational GoF text that defines the Strategy,
Observer and Decorator patterns; an empirical Java-based study demonstrating quantifiable

improvements from pattern adoption in e-commerce; and a structural analysis applying the

Decorator pattern to manage overlapping business rules without sub-class explosion.


1.4.1 Foundational Blueprints: Strategy, Observer, and Decorator Patterns

The work by the aforementioned Gang of Four (GoF) revolutionized the way modern systems

are engineered and architected. Published in 1994, their work outlined 23 design patterns that
encompass three categories: namely creational patterns (governing object instantiation),

behavioural patterns (governing interactions between objects while minimising coupling) and

structural patterns (governing class structures). Gamma et. al (1994) describe the practice of

adhering to design patterns as akin to an author following certain “tropes” in their writing,
such as the “Tragically Fallen Hero” in Hamlet and Macbeth (p. 19). Of direct relevance to

EZShop's discount processing requirements is the Strategy pattern, which is a behavioural

pattern that “defines a family of algorithms, encapsulates each one, and makes them
interchangeable. Strategy lets the algorithm vary independently from clients that use it" (p.

315). For an e-commerce platform like EZShop, the Strategy pattern is naturally suited to
discount calculation: percentage discounts, fixed-amount vouchers, seasonal rebates, and

tiered membership discounts can each be implemented as separate concrete strategy classes

conforming to a common interface, while the checkout context remains unchanged when new
discount types are introduced. Furthermore, the GoF also introduced the Observer pattern, a

behavioural pattern with the intent of defining a one-to-many dependency between objects so

that when one object changes state, all its dependents are notified and updated automatically
(p. 294). In EZShop, the Observer pattern could prove useful for real-time updates of order

status and notifications, by allowing all “observers” of a particular state to implement the

necessary functionalities associated with a change in the state they are observing. Moreover,
the Decorator pattern, sometimes known as a “wrapper”, seeks to attach additional

responsibilities to an object dynamically (p. 182). They note that decorators provide a flexible

alternative to subclassing for extending functionality. Within the context of EZShop, this

structural pattern is critical for handling overlapping promotions. Rather than creating a
convoluted inheritance tree for every possible combination of discounts (such as a

VipAndSeasonalDiscount subclass), the system can simply "wrap" a base checkout total with

multiple, independent decorator classes. Ultimately, the GoF catalog provides the theoretical
foundation for these three patterns, proving that encapsulating algorithms, decoupling state

notifications, and dynamically composing objects are universally recognized strategies for

building resilient systems.


1.4.2 Mitigating Complexity in Dynamic Pricing via the Strategy Pattern

Cappi et. al (2001) provides an architectural analysis of how to manage dynamic,

personalized business rules within e-commerce applications. The authors highlight that as
e-commerce     platforms evolve     to include personalized pricing, dynamic product

recommendations, and targeted promotional campaigns, the underlying logic often degrades

into deeply nested conditional statements. They specifically note that attempting to code

personalized discount algorithms through traditional subclass wrappers or massive “case”
statements is an approach that "won't scale up and the code ends completely messed up" (p.

463). To resolve this technical debt, the study advocates for a clear separation of concerns by

implementing the Strategy pattern as defined by the GoF. In their proposed architecture, the
customer profile delegates the behaviour for obtaining a discount to an external object, rather
than embedding the calculation in the core system. This allows the e-commerce platform to
dynamically trigger execution after receiving the corresponding algorithm. As a result,

different algorithms can be chosen depending on the specific customer context, and these

algorithms can even be swapped at runtime as the user's profile or promotional eligibility
evolves. Their work validates the feasibility of the Strategy pattern in calculating discounts

seamlessly.


1.4.3 Managing Overlapping Promotions and Modularity

Charalampidou et. al (2017) present an analysis of the Decorator pattern's impact on software

quality attributes, demonstrating that its effectiveness depends heavily on the context of its

application. Using a theoretical model based on structural design metrics, the authors note
that while static instances of the pattern can decrease system cohesion, applying the

Decorator in contexts requiring dynamic, incremental extension significantly enhances

modularity and extensibility. For an e-commerce platform like EZShop, where multiple
overlapping promotions such as percentage discounts, fixed-amount vouchers, and seasonal

rebates, must be applied simultaneously, the Decorator pattern offers an architectural

solution. Rather than relying on various specialized subclasses to handle every possible
discount combination, each promotion can be implemented as a concrete decorator that

dynamically wraps a base price calculation at runtime. This strictly adheres to the

Open-Closed Principle, allowing new discount types and ordered calculation chains to be
integrated without modifying existing logic. However, the authors also warn of structural

trade-offs: if the system is extended by adding concrete decorators that merely inherit

functionalities without offering class-specific operations, design quality and understandability
actually diminish. Consequently, these findings directly validate EZShop's strategy to use the

Decorator pattern for stacking promotions, provided that the architectural application remains

disciplined and avoids unnecessary over-decoration.


1.5. Project and System Scope

The system is designed as a web-based application to ensure high availability without

requiring platform-specific installations. The frontend is built with a responsive web interface
to support access from desktop and mobile browsers.
1.5.1 In-Scope Capabilities

The system is designed to handle the following:


    ● Catalog and shopping cart management: The system will allow customers to

       browse the e-commerce clothing catalog, view product details, and manage their
       shopping cart (add, remove, and update item quantities) prior to checkout.

    ● Checkout and discount processing: The system will allow users to apply multiple

       types of promotions during the checkout phase. The system will dynamically calculate
       complex pricing combinations, such as stacking a seasonal percentage sale with a

       fixed-amount shipping voucher to generate an accurate final price. Credit card, online

       banking and e-wallet are accepted as payment options.

    ● Automated order processing and notifications: The system will manage the
       lifecycle of a customer's order. Upon a successful checkout, the system will

       automatically trigger real-time background processes to deduct purchased items from

       the inventory and send automated order confirmation notifications to the customer.
    ● Scalable promotional management: The system is designed to allow administrators

       to easily integrate new discount schemes, marketing campaigns, or pricing rules in the

       future without disrupting the existing core checkout functionalities.


1.5.2 Out-of-Scope Capabilities

To maintain a clear focus on implementing software design patterns, the following

functionalities are out of the scope for this system:

    ● Live payment gateway integration: The system will simulate payment processing

       transactions. It will not integrate with live third-party financial APIs.
    ● User authentication: The system assumes a “guest checkout” model, meaning that

       customers will not be required to create an account to purchase items. User

       registration and authentication are excluded from this system design.
    ● Delivery and logistics tracking: Advanced fleet management, GPS tracking, and

       integration with third-party courier services are excluded. Order status will simply

       update to generic states internally.
    ● Comprehensive Identity Management & RBAC: The public storefront operates

       strictly on a frictionless "guest checkout" model; customer account creation and

       profile  management    are excluded. For internal      staff, the integration of
       enterprise-grade Identity Providers (IdPs), OAuth authorization servers, and complex

       Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) are out of scope. The architecture relies on a
       localized, lightweight access boundary to separate administrative domains from public

       traffic, prioritizing backend pattern implementation over complex user provisioning.

    ● Multi-currency processing and tax calculations: All transactions are assumed to be
       processed in Ringgit Malaysia (RM). Dynamic tax calculation logic based on

       geographic shipping regions is explicitly excluded.

    ● Recommendation         engines:    Machine     learning   algorithms    for   product
       recommendations based on user browsing history are beyond the scope of this

       architectural design document.


1.5.3 Business Rules


1. Account and Platform Policies


    ● B2C Retail Model: EZShop operates exclusively as a single-vendor retail platform. It
       is not a multi-vendor marketplace; all inventory is owned and managed centrally by

       EZShop Admins.

    ● Guest Checkout: The platform operates strictly on a guest-checkout model. User

       registration, account creation, and persistent login sessions are not required, and
       customer details are tied uniquely to individual order instances.

    ● Currency Used in Transactions: All financial transactions, base prices, and

       fixed-amount discounts are calculated and processed exclusively in Ringgit Malaysia
       (RM).


2. Inventory and Shopping Cart Rules


    ● Real-Time Stock Validation: A customer cannot add an item to the cart if the
       requested quantity exceeds the physical stock available in the database. The system

       will auto-correct the requested quantity to the maximum available stock.

    ● Cart Abandonment: Shopping cart session data for unauthenticated guest users is
       retained in the system cache for a maximum of 24 hours before being purged.

    ● Checkout Inventory Lock: Inventory is verified a final time immediately before the

       payment gateway is invoked. If concurrent user activity depletes the stock before

       payment submission, the checkout process is aborted to prevent overselling.
3. Promotional and Pricing Rules (The Pricing Engine)


    ● Discount Value Constraints and Limits: Administrators cannot create a
       percentage-based discount greater than 100%, nor can they set negative stock values

       or negative base prices.

    ● Promotional Stacking: Customers may stack global marketing campaigns (e.g.,

       Seasonal Sales) with user-applied promotional vouchers (e.g., Shipping Discounts) on
       a single order, facilitated by the Decorator pattern.

    ● Order of Application: To maintain financial consistency, percentage-based discounts

       must be applied to the cart subtotal before fixed-amount deductions (e.g., an RM 5.00
       voucher) are subtracted.

    ● Minimum Spend Threshold Checks: Certain promotional vouchers require the cart's

       base subtotal to reach a specific minimum threshold. If the cart falls below this

       threshold (due to item removal), the system automatically detaches the promotion
       decorator from the checkout session.


4. Payment and Post-Purchase Rules


    ● Payment Methods Abstraction: The system must treat all payment methods (Credit
       Card, Online Banking, E-Wallet) interchangeably. The core checkout process must not

       contain provider-specific API logic.

    ● Notification Isolation: Post-purchase notifications (like email receipts) and inventory

       deductions must only execute if the payment gateway returns a "Success"
       authorization code.

    ● Fault Tolerance in Observers: If a non-critical secondary process (e.g., the Email

       Dispatcher) throws an exception during post-purchase execution, it must be caught

       locally and must not roll back the successful payment transaction or the inventory
       deduction.

2.2. Class Diagram


The EZShop architecture is divided into four main subsystems. This modular design ensures
the system is easy to maintain and strictly follows object-oriented principles.


    ● Core Domain: This holds the basic data models. A ShoppingCart contains multiple
       CartItems. Each CartItem links to a Product to check its price and available stock.

       Once a transaction is finalized, an Order is created to store the final total and status.
    ● Pricing Engine (Decorator & Strategy Patterns): This handles the discount

       calculations. First, BaseCartPricing gets the raw cart subtotal. To apply promotions, a
       PromotionDecorator "wraps" that total. The actual math (like 20% off or RM 5 off) is

       handed off to the DiscountStrategy interface. This setup allows the system to stack

       multiple different discounts on top of each other without relying on massive, messy

       if/else statements.
    ● Payment Processing (Strategy Pattern): This handles the checkout phase. The

       CheckoutContext delegates the transaction to a PaymentStrategy interface. Because

       the system just looks at the interface, it doesn't care whether the user is paying via
       CreditCardPayment, EWalletPayment, or OnlineBankingPayment. This makes adding

       new payment methods in the future seamless.

    ● Order Notifications (Observer Pattern): This manages the background tasks after a

       successful payment. Once the OrderProcessor (the Subject) marks an order as "Paid",
       it automatically notifies its list of Observers. The InventoryObserver steps in to

       deduct the purchased stock, and the EmailNotificationObserver sends the customer

       their receipt. This keeps the checkout process fast and entirely separated from
       post-purchase tasks.

3. Requirements


3.1. Software Design Concepts and Design Principles

The system architecture of EZShop has used software design concepts and adheres to SOLID

principles.


3.1.1 Software Design Concepts applied

Modularity


Definition:

Modularity is the practice of breaking large, complex systems into smaller, independent, and
interchangeable components called modules. Each of the modules are designed to perform a

specific function, and can be tested and maintained independently.


How is it applied into EZShop:
EZShop has been separated into distinct, manageable parts, which is Core Domain, Pricing

Engine, Payment Processing, and Order Notifications. By applying this concept, it helps to
avoid having a complex structure and allow developers to maintain each subsystem

subsequently. For example, if a request is made for a massive haul of the promotional

campaigns, the developer can modify the Pricing Engine module safely without risking any
disruption to the Payment Processing or core Domain modules.



Functional Independence


Definition:

Functional independence is a design principle where modules and components are designed
to perform a single and specific task, and minimize the interaction with other parts of the

system.


How is it applied in EZShop:

By implementing the Observer pattern, the checkout module remains decoupled from
post-purchase tasks. When an order is made, the OrderProcessor will just perform its work by

updating the status to “paid”. Other than that, other modules like InventoryObserver are
highly cohesive, performing singular and focus tasks without relying on the internal workings

of the other systems. Because of its functional independence, an error formatting the receipt
inside the EmailNotificationObserver will never cause the inventory deduction to fail.


Encapsulation


Definition:

Encapsulation means that modules should be specified and designed so that the information

,such as the algorithm and data, contained within a module is inaccessible to other modules
that had no need for such information.



How is it applied in EZShop:

Encapsulation is implemented in EZShop to hide complex algorithms from modules that do
not require them. The API logic that requires communication with external payment

gateways (like banks and e-wallets) is encapsulated within PaymentStrategy classes. The

main CheckoutContext is shielded from these external details. This prevents sensitive
payments logic from leaking into the core application and isolates API-related errors.




3.1.2 SOLID Design Principles applied


Single Responsibility Principle (SRP)


SRP dictates that a class should have a single, well-defined responsibility. In EZShop, the
EmailNotificationObeserver handles only customer contact logic, ensuring that updates to the

email formatting cannot break the inventory deduction system.


Without SRP:

       As can be seen in the code below, the functions responsible for stock deduction and

       email notification are all in the same class.
       //   VIOLATION: One class with two unrelated responsibilities
       class OrderCompletionHandler {

            public void handleOrderCompletion(String orderId, double
       finalTotal, String status) {


                 System.out.println("Deducting stock for order: " +
       orderId);
              System.out.println("Sending receipt for: " + orderId);
              System.out.printf("Total: RM %.2f%n", finalTotal);

          }
      }




SRP applied:

      As can be seen in the code below, the stock deduction and email notification has now

      been divided into two classes which is InventoryObserver for stock deduction and

      EmailNotificationObserver for email notification function. By separating these two

      into different classes, changing the email receipt format will only need to modify

      EmailNotificationObserver. The Inventory deduction logic will be completely

      unaffected and eliminate regression risk between both unrelated functions.

Open/Closed Principle (OCP)


OCP ensures that software entities should be open for extension but closed for modification.

In EZShop, the Pricing Engine utilizes the Decorator Pattern. For example, when a new
campaign    is  launched,  developers   can  inject     a  new    concrete  decorator

(MembershipTierDecorator) without altering the existing BaseCartPricing code and eliminate

the risk of bugs.

   -   Without applying OCP:

       As can be seen in the code, if a new promotion needs to be added, the class is required

       to be edited.
       //   VIOLATION: Every new promotion forces editing this class

            public double calculateTotal(double subtotal, String
       promoType) {

                     return subtotal * 0.80; // 20% off

                     return subtotal - 5.00; // RM 5.00 off
                }

                return subtotal;
            }

       As can be seen in the codeMembershipTierDecoratois added as a new class without

       // OCP: Each new promotion is a new class — existing classes
       never modified.


       interface IPricingComponent {
            double calculateTotal();

       }


       // OCP: Closed for modification — this class never changes
       class BaseCartPricing implements IPricingComponent {

            private double cartSubtotal;
            public BaseCartPricing(double cartSubtotal) {
        this.cartSubtotal = cartSubtotal;
    }

    @Override
    public double calculateTotal() {

        System.out.printf(" [BaseCartPricing] Raw subtotal : RM
%.2f%n", cartSubtotal);

        return cartSubtotal;
    }

}


// OCP: HolidaySaleDecorator — closed for modification

class HolidaySaleDecorator extends PromotionDecorator {
    private static final double DISCOUNT_PERCENTAGE = 0.20;

    public HolidaySaleDecorator(IPricingComponent
wrappedComponent) {

        super(wrappedComponent);
    }

    @Override
    public double calculateTotal() {

        double before = wrappedComponent.calculateTotal();

Liskov Substitution Principle (LSP)


The Liskov Substitution Principle states that objects of a subclass must be substitutable for

their superclass without altering the correctness of the program. Any concrete implementation

injected into a context must honor the expected contract. In the payment processing module,

every concrete payment method (such as EWallet and OnlineBanking) guarantees it will
honor the contract of the PaymentStrategy interface.This ensures that the main checkout

context can safely swap payment types without crashing.

3.1.3 Software Design Considerations


   a. Scalability and Performance


      The system must be able to handle high volumes of transactions simultaneously,
      especially during seasonal sales or holiday sales. The Decorator patterns used in the

      Pricing Engine allows the system to stack discounts in memory during runtime

      without requiring multiple database calls or instantiating massive inheritance tree.

      However, the system must manage the object life cycle actively to prevent memory

      leaks when a lot of people are doing their checkout period.
b. Maintainability and Readability


    By adhering to this design principle, the resulting software will be more easy to

    maintain, modify and troubleshoot. This approach ensures the codebase to be more
    organized and easy to understand. In EZShop, this reduces the time and effort for

    future bug fixes or module enhancements. High readability also means that each team

    member can easily understand the architecture and continue to do their part on the
    project.



c. Reliability and Consistency


    System reliability is very important especially for an e-commerce platform handling

    live financial transactions. By adhering to this software principle, the software can

    achieve a higher standard for quality, making the system to be more reliable, secure
    and a better performance. For example by enforcing Liskov Substitution

    Principle(LSP) within the payment processing module, the software can remain more

    consistent and reliable when new subclasses of payments method are introduced in the
    future.
4. Proposed Design Patterns


a. Strategy Pattern


i. Function or Software Component Affected

EZShop’s Checkout and Payment Processing module, along with its Discount and Price

Calculation module subsystems apply the Strategy Pattern, both of which operate in tandem
to ensure the system’s financial accuracy and transactional security. The pattern resolves the

issue of coupling the core checkout flow with variable dependencies, such as third-party
payment gateways, or complex internal business logic like discounts and promotions.



In the Payment Processing module, the pattern defines a family of algorithms for transaction
routing. The PaymentStrategy interface establishes the contract for all concrete payment

methods, mandating a unified method for processing transactions. The CheckoutContext

class assumes the role of the Context, managing the order total and delegating the secure
transaction routing to the currently selected concrete strategy implementation. Concrete

strategy   classes,   such   as    CreditCardPayment,       OnlineBankingPayment,        and

EWalletPayment, each encapsulate the unique API logic required to simulate
communication with their respective external payment providers.



The pattern is also essential to the Dynamic Price Calculation module, where it abstracts the
diverse mathematical rules for promotions. The IDiscountStrategy interface defines the core

mathematical function for calculating price reductions, allowing the system to use different

algorithms without altering the underlying pricing structure. This strategy is primarily
leveraged by the PromotionDecorator (which acts as a context within the Decorator pattern

structure), delegating the specific calculation to an injected strategy object. Concrete strategy
classes like PercentageStrategy and FixedAmountStrategy implement IDiscountStrategy,

ensuring that new promotional rules can be added effortlessly.


ii. Description of work flow or data flow

The Payment Strategy workflow begins when the user selects their preferred payment

method (e.g., Credit Card, Online Banking, eWallet) via the user interface during checkout.
Based on this selection, the client (frontend controller) instantiates the specific concrete

strategy object corresponding to that method. This strategy object, along with the order
payload (total amount, user ID), is then passed into the main Payment Context class. The

Payment Context's primary function is delegation: it bypasses complex conditional logic by
calling a single, unifiedProcessPayment() method on the injected strategy interface.

The concrete strategy then communicates with its specific external API, processes the

transaction, and returns a boolean (Success/Fail) back through the Context to the user

interface.1

The Discount Strategy workflow is integrated with the Decorator Pattern to handle complex

price reductions. The process is initiated when the customer submits a promotional code or

the system identifies an active global marketing campaign. The backend CheckoutContext

first fetches the raw, undiscounted cart subtotal directly from the BaseCartPricing component.
The system subsequently maps the specific promotion to its corresponding concrete strategy

object (e.g., a percentage discount or a fixed-amount voucher). To enable the stacking of

promotions,    the   base   pricing   component     is   dynamically    wrapped     inside   a

PromotionDecorator. The PromotionDecorator then delegates the mathematical calculation to
the injected DiscountStrategy interface by passing the subtotal, thereby maintaining a clear

separation of concerns and avoiding nested conditional logic. The concrete strategy object

executes its specific mathematical algorithm (e.g., calculating 20% off) and returns the new
value, allowing this delegation process to loop sequentially for multiple promotions before

returning the accurately calculated final price to the frontend interface.

In this design, the CheckoutContext acts as the context, it doesn't hardcode specific payment

additional payment methods are added, keeping the system stable and highly maintainable.


The PaymentStrategy serves as the Abstract Strategy, acting as a formal contract that
dictates how all payment methods must behave. By defining a unified

processPayment() signature, it allows the system to treat vastly different

implementations—like CreditCardStrategy, OnlineBankingStrategy, and

EWalletStrategy—as interchangeable components. These Concrete Strategies contain the
specific, encapsulated logic for their respective providers. For instance, the EWalletStrategy

might handle the specific API handshakes for local services like GrabPay or Touch 'n Go,
while the CreditCardStrategy focuses on secure gateway encryption, all without the rest of

the application needing to know those technical details.















Discount Strategy
Similarly, the Discount Strategy utilises the PromotionContext as a secondary context. This

class holds a reference to a DiscountStrategy, which acts as the Abstract Strategy for price
adjustments. The Concrete Strategies, such as PercentageStrategy and

FixedAmountStrategy, perform the actual math—calculating a fraction of the total or

subtracting a flat Ringgit amount, respectively. By wrapping this logic in a decorator, you

allow the system to dynamically calculate totals while potentially "stacking" or wrapping
additional promotional rules around the base discount logic.


Finally, the Client acts as the orchestrator of the entire ecosystem, responsible for "wiring"

the components together at runtime. It instantiates the specific concrete strategies based on
user input and injects them into the respective contexts. This approach perfectly exemplifies

the Open/Closed Principle: the system is open for extension (you can add a

"CryptoPayment" or "SeasonalDiscount" easily) but closed for modification (you never have
to change the CheckoutContext code itself). This design not only makes your codebase

more professional but also makes unit testing significantly easier, as each strategy can be

tested in total isolation.

vi. Benefit

The principal architectural advantage of implementing the Strategy Pattern in EZShop is the
rigorous compliance it enforces with the Open/Closed Principle. This is one of the most

significant benefits for an e-commerce platform where the system must be open for extension

but closed for modification. New functionalities, such as incorporating payment methods like
Apple Pay or GrabPay, or integrating new discount types and promotions, can be achieved by

simply creating a new class that implements the original strategy interface without requiring

any changes to the existing, proven core codebase.


The pattern also achieves crucial Separation of Concerns. The complex API logic necessary

for communicating with external payment gateways is isolated entirely within individual

concrete strategy classes. This architecture prevents the main e-commerce checkout
workflow from becoming cluttered and simplifies maintenance by decoupling the core

system from external dependencies.


Furthermore, the Strategy Pattern ensures crucial Interchangeability. Both payment

processing and discount algorithms can be swapped dynamically at runtime. This adaptability

can be driven by various factors, including customer input (choosing a payment method),
regional availability of a service, or the customer's membership subscriptions, allowing the

system to handle varied transactional requirements instantly.



Finally, it significantly facilitates Isolated Unit Testing. Both the payment and discount
algorithms, now encapsulated in their own strategy classes, can be independently tested

without the complex overhead of implementing databases, user sessions, or full checkout

sequences. This allows for the creation of fast, reliable unit tests that feed raw data directly
into the strategies, ensuring mathematical and procedural correctness efficiently.



vii. Limitations
While highly effective for keeping algorithms decoupled, the Strategy pattern does introduce

a few architectural trade-offs, such as Class Proliferation (Class Explosion). Because the

pattern strictly mandates that every unique algorithm must be encapsulated within its own
dedicated class, the overall codebase can rapidly inflate. For instance, if the marketing

department introduces dozens of highly specific, one-off variations for discounts, the backend

structure will quickly become cluttered with numerous concrete strategy classes. This
proliferation directly increases the cognitive load required for developers to navigate and

understand the directory structure.


A second significant limitation involves Data Payload Bottlenecks. The DiscountStrategy

interface   defines   a  rigid   contract   for  the   data it    expects to receive      (e.g.,
calculateDiscount(cartSubtotal)), limiting its flexibility. If a future, more sophisticated

discount scheme requires new contextual data for its calculation—such as the user's

geographic location or their lifetime purchase history—the interface signature must be
modified. This change forces an update across all existing concrete strategies, which

unintentionally violates the Open/Closed Principle during the necessary refactoring process.


Finally, the pattern imposes a Client Awareness Burden. The system component responsible

for instantiating the correct concrete strategies (which is often a factory or the promotion

configuration module) must be intimately aware of the purpose and operational differences of

each individual strategy. If the module's logic for mapping a promotion to its corresponding
class is flawed, the system could easily inject an incompatible or incorrect mathematical

algorithm into the checkout flow, leading to pricing errors.






b. Observer Pattern


i. Function or Software Component Affected
EZShop's Order Notifications and Post-Purchase Processing subsystems use the Observer

Pattern. When a successful payment transaction is confirmed, this subsystem controls the

automatic background processes that need to run quickly and consistently. A strongly
connected system where the basic checkout logic handles unrelated issues might come from

the checkout module having to directly invoke each post-purchase service, such as inventory

management and customer notification, in the absence of an organized architectural approach.


The following components are directly impacted by this pattern. The IOrderSubject interface

exposes the attachObserver(IOrderObserver observer), detachObserver(IOrderObserver
observer), and notifyObservers(String orderId, double finalTotal, String status) methods,

which specify the formal contract for the observable object. The actual implementation of this
interface is the OrderProcessor class, which acts as the pattern's Subject. It maintains a

private final list of IOrderObserver instances named observers and a private String field
named    currentOrderId.    When    it   receives  a   payment    success    signal  via   its

completeOrder(String orderId, double finalTotal) method, it changes the order status to

"Paid" and then invokes notifyObservers.


The observer contract is defined by the IOrderObserver interface, which mandates that all

implementing classes supply a concrete implementation of the update(String orderId, double
finalTotal, String status) method. The InventoryObserver class, a concrete observer, would

contain logic to decrease stock quantities. The EmailNotificationObserver class, a second

concrete observer, formats and sends an order confirmation receipt to the customer. These
elements work together to create a fully decoupled event-driven notification architecture that

separates the particulars of post-purchase processing from the checkout process.




ii. Description of work flow or data flow
The Observer Pattern is based on the principle of a one-to-many dependency, in which a

single Subject object automatically updates and tells all dependent Observer objects when its

internal state changes, without the Subject needing to be directly aware of what those
observers do. This corresponds to the following end-to-end workflow in the context of

EZShop.



The appropriate concrete observer instances, namely an InventoryObserver and an
EmailNotificationObserver, are instantiated and registered with the OrderProcessor by

invoking the attachObserver method during system initialization or session setup. Upon the

customer's completion of the payment process, the CheckoutContext invokes the
completeOrder(String orderId, double finalTotal) method on the OrderProcessor. Inside this

method, the processor executes two actions in succession. Initially, it prints a confirmation

message including the order ID and the status "Paid". Subsequently, it promptly runs its own
notifyObservers(String orderId, double finalTotal, String status) method. This internal

function traverses all observers registered in the observers collection and calls the update

method on each, supplying the orderId, finalTotal, and status as data payloads.
Each observer subsequently implements its own autonomous logic separately. The

InventoryObserver would traverse a collection of cart items, obtain the corresponding product
for each item, compute the new stock level by deducting the purchased quantity, and save the

updated value. The EmailNotificationObserver retrieves pertinent order information such as

the Order ID and final total, and sends a structured confirmation message to the consumer.
Importantly, neither observer interacts with the other, and neither the OrderProcessor nor the

CheckoutContext possesses any knowledge of the internal operations of these observers. The

entire post-purchase workflow is executed through a single method call, ensuring a clear and
distinct separation of responsibilities inside the system.

import java.util.ArrayList;

import java.util.List;

// ─── IOrderObserver ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
// SRP: Defines one contract — the observer notification method only.
// OCP: Any new post-purchase behaviour implements this interface without

//      modifying OrderProcessor or any existing observer.

interface IOrderObserver {

   void update(String orderId, double finalTotal, String status);
}



// ─── IOrderSubject ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
// SRP: Defines one contract — the subject observer management methods only.

interface IOrderSubject {

   void attachObserver(IOrderObserver observer);
   void detachObserver(IOrderObserver observer);

   void notifyObservers(String orderId, double finalTotal, String status);

   void completeOrder(String orderId, double finalTotal);
}



// ─── OrderProcessor ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
// SRP: Responsible for one thing — managing order state transitions and

//      broadcasting that state change to registered observers. It does not

//      know or care what any observer does internally.

// OCP: Closed for modification. The completeOrder() and notifyObservers()
//      methods never need to change when new observer types are added.

class OrderProcessor implements IOrderSubject {

   private final List<IOrderObserver> observers = new ArrayList<>();
   private String currentOrderId;



   @Override
   public void attachObserver(IOrderObserver observer) {

       if (!observers.contains(observer)) {

           observers.add(observer);
       }

   }



   @Override
   public void detachObserver(IOrderObserver observer) {

       observers.remove(observer);

   }


   @Override

   public void notifyObservers(String orderId, double finalTotal, String status) {
       for (IOrderObserver observer : observers) {

           observer.update(orderId, finalTotal, status);

       }
   }
   public void completeOrder(String orderId, double finalTotal) {
       String status = "Paid";

       this.currentOrderId = orderId;

       System.out.println("[OrderProcessor] Order " + orderId

           + " status updated to '" + status + "'.");
       notifyObservers(orderId, finalTotal, status);

   }

}


// ─── InventoryObserver ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

// SRP: Responsible for one thing — stock deduction logic only.
//      Any change to inventory rules is isolated here and cannot affect

//      the email notification logic in EmailNotificationObserver.

   @Override

   public void update(String orderId, double finalTotal, String status) {

       System.out.println("\n[InventoryObserver] Processing stock deduction for
Order: " + orderId);

       System.out.println("   Order Status : " + status);

       System.out.println("   Order Total   : RM " + String.format("%.2f",

finalTotal));
       System.out.println("[InventoryObserver] Stock deduction complete.");

   }

}

//      the email formatting logic cannot break the inventory deduction system

//      because the two concerns are fully isolated in separate classes.

   @Override

       System.out.println("\n[EmailNotificationObserver] Preparing confirmation

       System.out.println("   |             EZShop Order Confirmation
|");

vi. Benefit

The principal architectural advantage of using the Observer Pattern in EZShop's

post-purchase subsystem is the realisation of authentic loose coupling between the checkout

process and its subsequent side effects. The OrderProcessor subject does not have a

compile-time reliance on either the InventoryObserver or the EmailNotificationObserver. Its

sole awareness of its observers is via the IOrderObserver interface contract. This indicates

that the complete post-purchase processing framework can be altered, expanded, or

substituted without necessitating modifications to the preceding checkout or payment logic.
The pattern mandates rigorous compliance with the Open/Closed Principle. As the EZShop

platform evolves and new post-purchase requirements arise, including an SMS notification
service, a loyalty points calculation engine, or a real-time analytics event emitter, each

requirement can be addressed by creating a new concrete observer class and registering it

with the OrderProcessor. The existing codebase remains unaltered, significantly reducing
regression risk. The provided code demonstrates this by attaching and detaching observers at

runtime without modifying the OrderProcessor class.


Moreover, each specific observer class exemplifies the Single Responsibility Principle. The

InventoryObserver     is   only   accountable    for   stock   management,     whereas    the

EmailNotificationObserver is solely tasked with customer contact. This clear decomposition
renders each class independently testable via unit testing, independently deployable, and

independently maintainable. An error in the email formatting logic can be identified and

rectified without the risk of unintentionally impacting the inventory management system.


The Observer Pattern furthermore offers runtime adaptability. As observers are registered and

deregistered via method calls instead of fixed dependencies, the system can dynamically alter

the active post-purchase procedures. During a designated maintenance time, the email
observer may be deregistered without disrupting inventory processing or necessitating a

system restart, as shown in the ObserverPatternDemo where detachObserver is called before

the second order.



vii. Limitations

Notwithstanding its structural benefits, the Observer Pattern presents certain architectural

trade-offs that require meticulous management inside the EZShop framework. The primary
issue is the lack of assured execution sequencing. Observers are informed in the order of their

registration with the Subject. The current implementation, while predictable, creates a

precarious implicit dependency: should a subsequent observer depend on a side effect
generated by a prior observer, the system's correctness would hinge on the order of

registration, an obscure and challenging constraint that may lead to subtle bugs.


A secondary problem is the possibility of memory leaks if observers are not explicitly

deregistered when they are no longer needed. The OrderProcessor retains strong references to
every registered observer inside its observers list. If observer objects are instantiated per
session or per request without invoking the necessary detachObserver method, the Subject

will maintain active references to objects that should have been eligible for garbage

collection, resulting in unbounded memory growth in long-running server processes.


The pattern further complicates debugging and traceability. The notification mechanism is

implicit, thus a developer examining the completeOrder method will see only one invocation
of notifyObservers. The tangible downstream impacts, such as inventory reduction and email

transmission, are not apparent at the call location. In intricate systems with numerous

registered observers, this indirection can substantially complicate the identification of a bug's
source, especially when the problem arises from an unforeseen interaction between two

observers responding to the same event.


Furthermore, the fundamental Observer architecture outlined does not ensure mistake

isolation among observers. If the InventoryObserver.update() method raises an unhandled

exception, causing the iteration loop in notifyObservers to conclude before executing
EmailNotificationObserver.update(), the customer might not receive their confirmation

receipt despite the acceptance of their payment. A production-grade implementation must

encapsulate each observer invocation behind distinct exception handling, hence introducing
complexity to the Subject class that exceeds the original scope of the pattern.



c. Decorator Pattern


i. Function or Software Component Affected
The Decorator Pattern is utilized in the Pricing Engine, particularly in the discount

calculation and promotional stacking subsystem of EZShop. This subsystem computes the

precise final price of a customer's shopping cart during checkout, considering any
concurrently applicable promotions. This pattern tackles the architectural difficulty of

dynamically composing numerous independent discount rules without relying on a rigid and

unmanageable inheritance tree.


The components immediately impacted are as follows. The IPricingComponent interface

establishes a standardized contract for all pricing entities, mandating that implementers
provide a single calculateTotal() method that returns a double indicating the current cart total.
The BaseCartPricing class serves as the concrete component and the fundamental ingredient

of each price chain. It accepts a double cartSubtotal parameter in its constructor and returns
that value when calculateTotal() is called. The PromotionDecorator abstract class functions as

the abstract decorator, implementing IPricingComponent and maintaining a reference to

another IPricingComponent instance named wrappedComponent, thereby facilitating
recursive composition. The concrete decorator classes, namely HolidaySaleDecorator,

ShippingVoucherDecorator, and MembershipTierDecorator, extend PromotionDecorator and

each incorporate a specific promotional discount into the computation sequence. The
HolidaySaleDecorator applies a 20% percentage discount, the ShippingVoucherDecorator

subtracts a fixed amount of RM 5.00, and the MembershipTierDecorator applies a

configurable      percentage      discount     passed      via     its     constructor     as
membershipDiscountPercentage.





ii. Description of work flow or data flow

The Decorator Pattern operates by enclosing objects that possess a shared interface within

successive layers of supplementary behaviour. Consequently, invoking a method on the
outermost wrapper initiates a series of delegated calls throughout the entire chain, with each

layer imparting its own alteration to the outcome before relaying it upward. In EZShop, this

approach enables the successive application of an unlimited number of promotional
reductions to a basic cart price, without any individual class requiring knowledge of the

others.


The workflow commences upon the initiation of the checkout procedure. A BaseCartPricing

object is created using a double value representing the customer's current cart subtotal, such

as RM 80.00. Upon direct invocation of calculateTotal() on this object, it yields the

unchanged subtotal, RM 80.00.


Upon consumer qualification for a promotional campaign, the system generates a decorator

chain encircling the base component. The initial relevant promotion, exemplified by a holiday
seasonal sale providing a 20% discount, is implemented by creating a HolidaySaleDecorator

and supplying the BaseCartPricing instance as an argument to its constructor. The decorator

internally retains this reference as its wrappedComponent. A second offer, a fixed-amount
shipping voucher reducing RM 5.00, is introduced by creating a ShippingVoucherDecorator

and providing the HolidaySaleDecorator instance as its constructor argument, so extending
the chain. A third offer, a membership tier discount of 10%, can be added by creating a

MembershipTierDecorator and placing it appropriately in the chain. The CheckoutContext

now contains a reference to this outermost decorator as an IPricingComponent.


Upon the invocation of calculateTotal() by the CheckoutContext on this outermost reference,

recursive delegation ensues. The ShippingVoucherDecorator invokes calculateTotal() on its
encapsulated component. This continues until the BaseCartPricing calculates and yields RM

80.00. The MembershipTierDecorator receives this amount and applies its percentage

discount, returning the new total. The HolidaySaleDecorator receives that amount and applies
its 20% discount. The ShippingVoucherDecorator then subtracts RM 5.00, resulting in a final

total which is subsequently returned to the CheckoutContext. During this procedure, the

CheckoutContext executed a solitary method call via a single interface reference, entirely

oblivious to the intricacies or structure of the invoked chain.

v. Sample Potential Code
// ─── IPricingComponent ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

// SRP: Defines one contract — the calculateTotal() method only.
// OCP: Both BaseCartPricing and PromotionDecorator implement this interface.

//      New pricing behaviours are added by implementing this interface,
//      never by modifying it.
interface IPricingComponent {
   double calculateTotal();

}



// ─── BaseCartPricing ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
// SRP: Responsible for one thing — computing the raw undiscounted cart

//      subtotal only. It has no knowledge of any promotional logic.

// OCP: This class is closed for modification. No matter how many new
//      decorator types are added to the system, BaseCartPricing never changes.

class BaseCartPricing implements IPricingComponent {

   private double cartSubtotal;


   public BaseCartPricing(double cartSubtotal) {

       this.cartSubtotal = cartSubtotal;
   }



   @Override
   public double calculateTotal() {

       System.out.printf("   [BaseCartPricing]               Raw subtotal

: RM %.2f%n",

           cartSubtotal);
       return cartSubtotal;

   }

}


// ─── PromotionDecorator ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

// SRP: Responsible for one thing — holding the wrapping reference to another
//      IPricingComponent and defining the abstract decoration contract.

//      It delegates the actual discount logic to each concrete subclass.

// OCP: Closed for modification. New promotion types extend this class
//      without requiring changes to it.

abstract class PromotionDecorator implements IPricingComponent {

   protected final IPricingComponent wrappedComponent;


   protected PromotionDecorator(IPricingComponent wrappedComponent) {

       this.wrappedComponent = wrappedComponent;

   }


   @Override

   public abstract double calculateTotal();
}



// ─── HolidaySaleDecorator ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
// SRP: Responsible for one thing — applying the 20% holiday sale discount only.

//      Any change to the holiday sale rate is isolated entirely within this class.
class HolidaySaleDecorator extends PromotionDecorator {
   private static final double DISCOUNT_PERCENTAGE = 0.20;



   public HolidaySaleDecorator(IPricingComponent wrappedComponent) {

       super(wrappedComponent);
   }



   @Override
   public double calculateTotal() {

       double totalBeforeDiscount = wrappedComponent.calculateTotal();

       double totalAfterDiscount   = totalBeforeDiscount * (1.0 -
DISCOUNT_PERCENTAGE);

       System.out.printf("   [HolidaySaleDecorator]          Applied 20%% holiday

sale discount       : RM %.2f --> RM %.2f%n" ,
           totalBeforeDiscount, totalAfterDiscount);

       return totalAfterDiscount;

   }
}



// ─── ShippingVoucherDecorator ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

// SRP: Responsible for one thing — applying the fixed RM 5.00 shipping
//      voucher deduction only. Changes to the shipping voucher amount

//      are isolated entirely within this class.

class ShippingVoucherDecorator extends PromotionDecorator {
   private static final double DISCOUNT_AMOUNT = 5.00;



   public ShippingVoucherDecorator(IPricingComponent wrappedComponent) {
       super(wrappedComponent);

   }


   @Override

   public double calculateTotal() {

       double totalBeforeDiscount = wrappedComponent.calculateTotal();

       double totalAfterDiscount   = totalBeforeDiscount - DISCOUNT_AMOUNT;
       System.out.printf("   [ShippingVoucherDecorator]      Applied RM 5.00 shipping

voucher        : RM %.2f --> RM %.2f%n" ,

           totalBeforeDiscount, totalAfterDiscount);
       return totalAfterDiscount;

   }

}


// ─── MembershipTierDecorator ──────────────────────────────────────────────────

// SRP: Responsible for one thing — applying a configurable membership
//      tier percentage discount only.

// OCP: This class demonstrates OCP in action. It was added as a new
//      campaign requirement by extending PromotionDecorator, without
//      modifying BaseCartPricing, HolidaySaleDecorator,

//      ShippingVoucherDecorator, or any other existing class.

class MembershipTierDecorator extends PromotionDecorator {

   private final double membershipDiscountPercentage;


   public MembershipTierDecorator(IPricingComponent wrappedComponent,

                                   double membershipDiscountPercentage) {
       super(wrappedComponent);

       this.membershipDiscountPercentage = membershipDiscountPercentage;

   }


   @Override

   public double calculateTotal() {
       double totalBeforeDiscount = wrappedComponent.calculateTotal();

       double totalAfterDiscount   = totalBeforeDiscount * (1.0 -

membershipDiscountPercentage);
       System.out.printf("   [MembershipTierDecorator]       Applied %.0f%%

membership discount         : RM %.2f --> RM %.2f%n" ,

           membershipDiscountPercentage * 100, totalBeforeDiscount,

totalAfterDiscount);
       return totalAfterDiscount;

   }

}


// ─── DecoratorPatternDemo (Main) ──────────────────────────────────────────────

public class DecoratorPatternDemo {
   public static void main(String[] args) {



       System.out.println("=== EZShop Pricing Engine Demo ===");
       System.out.println("   Cart subtotal before any promotions: RM 80.00\n");



       // Scenario 1: No promotions

       // SRP: BaseCartPricing is invoked alone, proving it has no
       //       knowledge of or dependency on any discount logic

       System.out.println("--- Scenario 1: No promotions (base price only) ---");

       IPricingComponent scenario1 = new BaseCartPricing(80.00);
       double total1 = scenario1.calculateTotal();

       System.out.printf("   Final Total: RM %.2f%n%n", total1);


       // Scenario 2: Holiday Sale only (20% off)

       // OCP: HolidaySaleDecorator is wrapped around BaseCartPricing

       //       without modifying BaseCartPricing
       System.out.println("--- Scenario 2: Holiday Sale (20% off) ---");

       IPricingComponent scenario2 = new HolidaySaleDecorator(
           new BaseCartPricing(80.00)

       );
       double total2 = scenario2.calculateTotal();

       System.out.printf("   Final Total: RM %.2f%n%n", total2);


       // Scenario 3: Holiday Sale (20% off) + Shipping Voucher (RM 5.00 off)
       // OCP: ShippingVoucherDecorator is stacked on top without modifying

       //       HolidaySaleDecorator or BaseCartPricing

       System.out.println("--- Scenario 3: Holiday Sale (20% off) + Shipping
Voucher (RM 5.00 off) ---");

       IPricingComponent scenario3 = new ShippingVoucherDecorator(
           new HolidaySaleDecorator(

                new BaseCartPricing(80.00)
           )

       );

       double total3 = scenario3.calculateTotal();
       System.out.printf("   Final Total: RM %.2f%n%n", total3);


       // Scenario 4: Membership (10%) + Holiday Sale (20%) + Shipping Voucher (RM

5.00)
       // OCP: MembershipTierDecorator was introduced as a new campaign requirement

       //       by extending PromotionDecorator only — BaseCartPricing,
       //       HolidaySaleDecorator, and ShippingVoucherDecorator are untouched

       System.out.println("--- Scenario 4: Membership (10%) + Holiday Sale (20%) +

Shipping Voucher (RM 5.00) ---" );
       IPricingComponent scenario4 = new ShippingVoucherDecorator(

           new HolidaySaleDecorator(
                new MembershipTierDecorator(

                    new BaseCartPricing(80.00), 0.10
                )

           )

       );
       double total4 = scenario4.calculateTotal();

       System.out.printf("   Final Total: RM %.2f%n", total4);
   }

}




vi. Benefit

The primary architectural advantage of implementing the Decorator Pattern in EZShop's

Pricing Engine is its ability to mitigate the subclass explosion identified by the Gang of Four.


In the absence of this pattern, each distinct combination of active promotions would require a

separate    class,    such     as     a    HolidayAndShippingDiscount       class,    a
MembershipAndHolidayDiscount class, and so on. As the variety of promotion kinds

expands, the number of necessary classes increases combinatorially. The Decorator Pattern
simplifies this complexity by dynamically combining behaviours at runtime, necessitating

only one class for each promotion type, irrespective of the number of conceivable

combinations. The DecoratorPatternDemo clearly shows four different scenarios built from
the same small set of classes.



The pattern ensures rigorous compliance with the Open/Closed Principle in the pricing
subsystem. The MembershipTierDecorator was added as a new campaign requirement by

extending    PromotionDecorator     only.   BaseCartPricing,   HolidaySaleDecorator,     and

ShippingVoucherDecorator      remain   completely    untouched.   No    alterations  to  the
BaseCartPricing class, the PromotionDecorator base class, or any current concrete decorators

are necessary for new promotion types. This renders the pricing engine truly extendable to

adapt to changing marketing demands without risking regression of present capabilities.


The pattern also enforces the Single Responsibility Principle. BaseCartPricing is responsible

only for returning the base cart subtotal. HolidaySaleDecorator applies only a 20% holiday

discount. ShippingVoucherDecorator handles only the RM 5.00 shipping voucher deduction.
MembershipTierDecorator applies only the membership percentage discount. Changes to any

one discount logic are isolated entirely within that single class.


The CheckoutContext class engages with the whole price chain via the singular

IPricingComponent interface, remaining entirely indifferent to the chain's complexity or

structure. The CheckoutContext is independently tested and maintainable, as it is not coupled

with any specific discount logic.


vii. Limitations
The principal structural constraint of the Decorator Pattern is that the resultant final price is

contingent upon the sequence in which decorators are applied in the chain. Applying a 20%

discount to a base price of RM 80.00 prior to deducting a set RM 5.00 voucher results in RM
59.00, whereas applying the fixed deduction first followed by the percentage reduction yields

RM 75.00. The pattern lacks a means to enforce a canonical ordering, indicating that the

correct composition sequence is an implicit assumption that the developer must uphold

during development, as shown in the four scenarios where the ordering is manually
constructed. If the chain is improperly built, the system will yield a mathematically valid yet
commercially erroneous result without generating any mistake.



The pattern diminishes the transparency of the runtime object hierarchy, hence complicating
debugging. Upon examining the pricing engine reference within CheckoutContext during

runtime, a developer perceives solely the outermost decorator. Establishing the complete

sequence of implemented promotions and their intermediate outcomes necessitates manually
navigating the recursive delegation by stepping through each decorator's calculateTotal()

method, which is significantly more arduous than reviewing a straightforward list of applied

discounts in a more uncomplicated, non-pattern implementation.


Moreover, when the quantity of registered promotion kinds escalates, the number of small,

single-purpose decorator objects that need to be instantiated per checkout session rises
proportionately. Scenario 4 in the demonstration requires three decorator objects

(ShippingVoucherDecorator,      HolidaySaleDecorator,     and     MembershipTierDecorator)

wrapping a single BaseCartPricing instance. Although each individual object is lightweight, a
system handling a substantial volume of simultaneous checkout sessions may generate a

considerable quantity of ephemeral decorator objects, hence intensifying the burden on the

garbage collector in managed runtime settings.


The Decorator Pattern, as implemented, consolidates all price logic into a singular method

signature, calculateTotal(). If the IPricingComponent interface requires extension with
additional methods in the future, such as a getDiscountBreakdown() method that provides a

comprehensive line-by-line summary of applied discounts, all existing concrete decorators

(HolidaySaleDecorator, ShippingVoucherDecorator, MembershipTierDecorator) and the

abstract decorator base class (PromotionDecorator) must be modified to implement or
delegate the new method. This imposes a maintenance load that increases with the quantity of

decorator classes in the system, necessitating a consideration of the trade-off against the

extensibility advantages offered by the design.




5. Conclusion and Suggestion
The EZShop architectural design successfully validates the feasibility and critical importance

of strategic software design pattern implementation in a modern e-commerce platform. The
system achieves essential quality attributes—extensibility, maintainability, and loose

coupling—by applying the Strategy, Observer, and Decorator patterns, directly addressing

the core functional requirement of dynamically calculating complex, stacked promotional

discounts. The Strategy Pattern ensures a robust Separation of Concerns in both payment
processing and discount calculation, successfully decoupling the core checkout logic from

diverse algorithms. This ensures that the system is Open for Extension to new payment

methods and discount formulas without modifying existing code, aligning directly with the
Open/Closed Principle (OCP). Furthermore, the Decorator Pattern offers an elegant solution

to the inherent Class Proliferation problem in promotional stacking. By dynamically

wrapping the base cart price, the system can apply an unlimited combination of discounts at

runtime, allowing new promotions to be added seamlessly without altering the core pricing
structure, which also reinforces OCP. Finally, the Observer Pattern establishes Loose

Coupling between the high-priority checkout transaction and non-critical, post-purchase side

effects like inventory deduction and notification. This event-driven architecture guarantees
that the core payment path remains fast and isolated, allowing background tasks to execute

independently without introducing synchronous dependencies. The integration of these

patterns has resulted in a highly modular and resilient architecture, as demonstrated by the

detailed diagrams and code samples. While the limitations identified for each pattern
(Strategy's Client Awareness Burden, Observer's lack of guaranteed execution sequencing,

and Decorator's implicit ordering dependency) are inherent trade-offs, they have been duly

noted for mitigation during the development phase.5.2 Suggestion


Based on the architectural findings and the identified limitations, the following suggestion is

proposed to significantly enhance the robustness and long-term maintainability of the

EZShop platform: Implement a Centralized Policy Engine to Govern Decorator and
Strategy Ordering. The most significant risk to the system's financial integrity lies in the

dependence on correct, explicit ordering for both the Decorator chain (price calculation

sequence) and the Observer notification list (post-purchase execution). To mitigate the

Decorator's reliance on implicit sequence and the Observer's lack of guaranteed execution
order, a dedicated, lightweight Policy Engine module should be introduced. This engine

would serve two primary purposes. First, it would provide Discount Policy Enforcement

(Decorator) by defining a clear, configurable, and canonical sequence for applying discounts
(e.g., always apply fixed-amount vouchers after percentage-based sales). This engine would
assume the sole responsibility for constructing the Decorator chain, removing the risk of

correct ordering from the developer during runtime promotion application. Second, it would

manage Observer Priority Management by controlling the registration and execution order
of Observers in the OrderProcessor. Instead of relying on passive registration order, the

engine would register Observers based on a predefined priority, ensuring critical actions (like

Inventory Deduction) are always executed and wrapped in explicit error handling before
non-critical actions (like Email Notification). This additional layer of abstraction (the Policy

Engine) will elevate the system's structural integrity, transforming implicit ordering

assumptions into explicit, maintainable business rules, thereby further strengthening
compliance with the Single Responsibility Principle and reducing future debugging

complexity.
